Julian — full of life
The hospital that failed him

Julian was the kind of person who made everyone around him feel alive. He rode his bike everywhere. He'd show up unannounced and somehow make your whole day better. He had this laugh — you'd hear it across a room and you couldn't help but smile.

He was bitten by a venomous snake while out on a ride. He went to the hospital. He was conscious. He was fighting. He expected to be saved. We all expected him to be saved.

But then two things happened inside that hospital — two failures, both preventable, both documented — and Julian never came home.

The hospital under-dosed his anti-venom below FDA-approved protocols. His own doctor later admitted the dosage wasn't enough.

I tried to get him more.

— Julian's treating physician

Those five words say everything. The doctor knew. The hospital didn't give Julian what he needed to survive.

But it didn't stop there.

Julian had a tracheostomy tube. His father — watching his son in that hospital bed, terrified — asked the nurses to restrain Julian's hands so he couldn't accidentally pull the tube out. Standard medical practice for tracheostomy patients during sedation weaning.

The nurses looked his father in the eyes and promised they would restrain him. They didn't.

Julian pulled out his own tracheostomy tube. Without the restraints his father begged for. Without the restraints the nurses promised.

Julian died from complications that should never have happened.

Two failures. An under-dosed medication his doctor said wasn't enough. A broken promise to a father who did everything he could to protect his son. And a young man who went to a hospital to be saved and never walked out.

The Incident

Julian is bitten by a venomous snake

He goes to the hospital conscious, alert, and alive. He is expected to survive.

Failure #1

Anti-venom administered below FDA-approved protocols

The FDA-approved dose for CroFab is 4–6 initial vials. For ANAVIP, it's 10 vials. Julian received less than the minimum standard. His body needed more and the hospital didn't provide it.

"I tried to get him more."

— The treating physician's admission

The Broken Promise

Father begs for restraints. Nurses promise. Nurses don't follow through.

Julian's father asks the nursing staff to restrain his son's hands to prevent him from pulling out his tracheostomy tube. The nurses assure him they will. They never do.

A father did everything in his power to protect his son. The hospital looked him in the eyes and made a promise they didn't keep.

Failure #2

Julian self-decannulates without restraints

During sedation weaning — without the restraints his father requested and the nurses promised — Julian pulls out his own tracheostomy tube. Standard practice requires physical restraints during this exact stage.

The Result

Julian dies from preventable complications

He walked into the hospital to be saved from a snakebite. He died because the hospital failed him — twice.

This hospital is still operating. These protocols haven't changed. The next Julian could be someone you love.

Julian's family doesn't have the resources to take on a hospital system alone. No family should have to. But with enough people standing behind them, they can get the legal representation they need — not just for Julian, but to force the policy changes that prevent this from ever happening again.

This isn't about anger. It's about accountability. It's about making sure the next father who begs a hospital to protect his child doesn't have to learn they broke that promise.
